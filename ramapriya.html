Gen Z protests: Is it the new normal?

Are we witnessing something similar to the Arab Spring that toppled a series of governments? This is yet another series led by the so-called Gen Z, i.e., those born between 1997 and 2012.

As we near the end of 2025, a wave of Gen Z-led protests is taking up the front stage. While these protests may not have emerged for the same reasons, rather they bring out the common brewing distress in society. From the Philippines, Indonesia, Nepal, Madagascar, Kenya, Serbia, Morocco, to Peru, the Gen Z protests have swept across the globe.

Source: Klebher Vasquez/Getty Images

The common trend in these protests is that in the initial stages of demonstration, the Gen Z had a specific set of issues, but later it simmered down into long-term concerns such as corruption, unemployment, repression, rising crime, growing inequality and so on. It picturizes the snowball effect, where these protests list out their demand initially, but ultimately it challenges the status quo. In doing so, it has spared dozens of lives due to the brutality of security forces inflicting human costs on the economy. They are anarchic in nature without any leader and organized using social media platforms such as TikTok, Instagram, Discord and X. These protests are evidence that Gen Z is desperate for existing governance, so they’re reshaping their political interests to address the social unrest in the nation.

The economics of protests should be underscored, as it costs the economy both in the short and long run. On the microeconomic scale, it stalls business operations, disrupts the supply chain, causes stock market volatility, and decline in consumer sentiment. Decline in structural productivity, emergency spending on law enforcement, disbursement of stimulus packages in reconstruction of destroyed physical properties strains the national budget and the reduction of inflow of Foreign Direct Investment (FDI) are some of the macroeconomic consequences. The unnoticed economic loss is the human costs. The loss of the productive labour force and increased healthcare spending are the add-ons to the already declining economy. The loss of lives due to protests would spiral down into prolonged unrest and political instability, leading to an even more sluggish economy, not able to recover from its scars.
