India's AI Ambition Faces a Reality Test

Vidhyaa D,

Defence and Strategic Studies.

Artificial Intelligence is quickly becoming one of the most powerful forces shaping the global economy. The governments are racing to control the technology that will define future productivity and power. India signalled its readiness for this competition through the recent AI Summit in New Delhi, where policy makers and industry leaders presented AI as a central pillar of the country's economic strategy. This shows how India gearing up for the technological turning point. The current global environment makes this a challenge for India as there is a need to make this technological development little faster. Here, Artificial Intelligence depends not only on the innovations but also on competition, even as it becomes part of the national strategy. Countries are investing heavily to secure technological leadership.


India has notable strengths. It has huge number of engineers, a globally recognised IT-services industry and digital public infrastructure that operates at a massive scale. Platforms for identity verification and digital payments have already transforming the daily lives. The AI applications now extended to improving medical diagnosis, increasing agriculture efficiency, enhancing urban planning and supporting financial inclusion.


Yet many of India's export earnings come from service industries such as outsourcing and routine IT support. AI systems are increasingly capable of performing these tasks faster and at lower cost. If automation spreads rapidly, it could threaten jobs, particularly for young graduates. The concern is not whether AI will change work, it certainly will but whether the workforce can adapt quickly enough.


Another issue is dependence. Advanced AI systems rely on high-performance chips and data infrastructure. India still imports much of this hardware. Without strengthening domestic manufacturing and research, India may remain dependant on foreign technology providers, limiting its long-term strategic autonomy. The AI summit emphasised skill development, innovation funding and regulatory clarity. These steps are crucial. AI can boost productivity and create new industries, but only if education systems and policies support inclusive growth.


India's AI ambition reflects both opportunity and pressure. In a world where technology shapes economic strength, staying passive is not an option. Whether India becomes a leader in artificial intelligence or merely a consumer of it will depend on decisions made today.
