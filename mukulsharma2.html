INTERNATIONAL DAY OF AWARENESS OF FOOD LOSS AND WASTE (IDAFLW)

Every Year September 29 is observed as the International Day of Awareness of Food Loss and Waste (IDAFLW). India being the largest food producer also faces food loss and waste as a major challenge. In the year 2022 NABCONS (NABARD Consultancy Services) published a report named ‘Study to Determine Post-Harvest Losses of Agri Produces in India’, here the central government conducted the three round post-harvest survey across more than 50 crops. This report provides a detailed assessment of agricultural losses across India’s Supply Chains and proposes strategies to minimise them. To mark the importance of this day, in the article a short summary of the report is being shared to create awareness of this problem and the possible solution to minimise food loss and waste.

India’s dominance in global agricultural production can be seen as the leading producer of milk and one of the top producers of cereals, fruits, vegetables, and fish. Despite robust production growth, food grains and horticulture output increasing steadily between 2018 and 2021, post-harvest losses continue to erode both economic and nutritional value. The report demonstrates the quantitative and qualitative losses caused by spoilage, mishandling, insects, poor storage and inadequate drying can severely affect overall food availability. Thus improving post-harvest management is vital for national food security and farmer incomes. With this motive some key objectives were defined which includes evaluating ongoing policies, identifying region- and crop-specific bottlenecks, and recommending a national framework to reduce food losses. Framing this problem in the global context with inclusion of SDG Target 12.3 goal—"halving food waste and reducing losses by 2030"

While discussing the results from the survey, the report quantifies losses for fifty-four crops across fifteen agro-climatic zones, distinguishing farm-level operations from market-level inefficiencies. In cereals such as paddy, wheat, maize, and sorghum, losses ranged between 3.9 to 5.9 percent, largely due to manual harvesting and poor threshing methods. In pulses like chickpea and pigeon pea, losses exceeded six percent because of shattering during harvest and spillage in handling. Oilseed losses, particularly in soybean, reached up to 7.5 percent, arising from mechanical inefficiencies and inadequate storage. Among fruits, guava recorded a loss of over fifteen percent, while pineapple had the lowest at around six, emphasizing the vulnerability of highly perishable produce. Vegetables such as tomato and leafy crops were especially affected; tomato losses averaged more than eleven percent due to bruising, poor packaging, and limited cold storage. Plantation crops like coconut and sugarcane showed moderate losses between 3.7 and 7.3 percent, while spices like black pepper and chilli ranged from 1.3 to 6 percent. Animal products exhibited remarkably lower yet significant waste: 6 percent for eggs, nearly 9 percent for marine fish, 5.6 percent for poultry meat, and less than 1 percent for milk. Overall, perishable commodities like fruits, vegetables, and fisheries accounted for the highest proportion of post-harvest losses nationally. The report compares these results with earlier assessments by ICAR-CIPHET (2015), revealing a general improvement in loss reduction owing to infrastructure upgrades and technological adoption; however, savings remain suboptimal. From the result it can be emphasized on the modern harvest equipment, scientific storage, and cold chain expansion as crucial mitigative measures.

In this report arguments on reducing losses by even a single percentage point could save the nation an immense quantity of food and substantially increase farmers’ revenue without additional cultivation. The economic analysis essentially reframes post-harvest management as both a food security imperative and a macroeconomic opportunity. To minimise post harvest losses that are tailored specific to each sector, like for fruits and vegetables, it advocates the creation of integrated cold chains, pack houses, pre-cooling units, ripening chambers, and refrigerated transport to prolong shelf life. Training farmers in grading, packaging, and hygienic handling could dramatically curb spoilage. Cereals and oilseeds can benefit from mechanized harvesters, mobile threshers, moisture meters, and scientific warehousing systems with aeration control. Additionally, public policy must incentivize investment through soft credit, tax concessions, and public–private partnerships for infrastructure development.

With India’s population projected to surpass that of China, food demand is expected to rise steeply. While production has increased, the report stresses that it is no longer sufficient to rely solely on expanding acreage or yield; reducing post-harvest losses has become the most efficient path to ensuring food security. Each stage from harvesting to storage, from transportation to retail introduces potential inefficiencies that cumulatively erode national output. Streamlining these processes thus becomes both an agronomic and economic necessity. A holistic policy framework should integrate infrastructure creation, farmer training, financial incentives, and coordinated institutional support. Only through such multi-tiered reform can India ensure that the full potential of its immense agricultural output reaches consumers without waste. India possesses vast agricultural capability, but inefficiencies in post-harvest management significantly undermine its benefits. Through systematic measurement, economic valuation, and strategic recommendations, the study provides a roadmap for transforming the agri-food system.
