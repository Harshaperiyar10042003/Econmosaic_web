What does the Venezuela oil blockade mean for the global economy?

There is a high scale action sequence taking place in the Caribbean, the US Coast Guard (USCG) pulling up stunts in full action mode by chasing oil tankers, eliminating boats, and killing more than 100 people.

There are rising tensions in the Caribbean between the US and Venezuela, starting with complaints over migration and drug trafficking and now it’s all about oil and regime change. Venezuela, the South American nation, has the largest proven oil reserves in the world and exported a little more than 790,000 barrels of oil per day this year. This majority of oil exports goes to China and the rest is sold in the black market.

Oil is the economic lifeline of Venezuela, as it actively fills the national coffers. But turning tables upside down, on 16 December, 2025, the US President Donald Trump ordered a ‘total and complete’ blockade of sanctioned tankers carrying Venezuelan oil. In a nutshell, this blockade implies that no US-sanctioned oil tankers are allowed to carry the oil in or out of Venezuela, and if the USCG finds sanctioned tankers carrying Venezuelan oil, they have the authority to stop, seize or sanction the vessel. This is a big blow to the already fragile Venezuelan economy. In the recent weeks, oil tankers named ‘The Skipper’ and ‘The Centuries’ were seized by USCG and they’re now actively pursuing the third vessel named ‘Bella 1’ which evaded the USCG and fled into the Atlantic Ocean. These pressure tactics pursued by the US aim to slow down Venezuela’s already sluggish economy by draining its coffers and challenging the current status quo ie., the 12-year-long of Maduro regime. It is speculated that this is again a direct pressure built by the US on a foreign nation for a regime change. This escalation started off when Trump complained about issues of migration and drug trafficking from Venezuela to the US. In full swing mode, the US responded by eliminating dozens of boats, with more than 100 people reportedly killed so far. This oil blockade appears to be just the beginning, and it may get even worse over time.

Even though the oil exports of Venezuela accounts for less than one percent of the global oil supply, after Trump’s announcement on the Truth Social platform, the geopolitical uncertainty and growing fears in the oil market have surged the oil prices. Analysts predicts that this disruption of such an insignificant amount of oil supply would not have a huge impact on the global oil prices but on further escalation of tensions between the two nations could have serious repercussions for the global oil market.
