From Palm to Cane : The Economic Story Behind Tamil Nadu’s Sugar Shift

Jaggery is one of the finest sweeteners, mostly used in Eastern and Southern India. The Eastern Ghats in Orissa host the Soura tribals, who make this jaggery in cottage industries under the Government of Orissa enterprise. Palm jaggery is quite extensively used to make sweets and desserts in the East of India. Bengali sweets like rasgulla and sondesh, particularly, are made with liquid date-palm jaggery — fondly referred to as Nolen Gur. In Tamil Nadu as well, sweets like halwa and savoury items like paniyaram are made with palm jaggery, which is called karupatti in most Southern regions.

The reason why date-palm jaggery is a less popular sweetener across India may lie in the fact that cane sugar was abundantly available in the North and West for many years. This is, of course, due to the fact that sugar-cane industries were developed under imperial preference during British rule. This, in turn, adversely affected palm-gur production in India.

Historical and economic evidence shows that several factors triggered the downfall of palm jaggery:

1. Colonial sugar expansion:

British-era policies encouraged large-scale sugar-cane cultivation and the establishment of sugar mills across India. (R.C DUTT -The Economic History of India) These mills mass-produced sugar and jaggery at lower costs giving sugar-cane products a strong economic edge.

2. Industrialisation vs. tradition:

Palm jaggery required skilled tree climbers, seasonal tapping, and manual processing. As Tamil Nadu modernised, labourers shifted to safer, higher-paying jobs. Sugar factories, in contrast, offered efficiency, steady supply, and uniform quality.

3. Consumer shift toward “clean” looking products:

Market studies show that consumers began preferring light-coloured sugar and sugar-cane jaggery over the darker, more rustic-looking palm jaggery. Hygiene perception played a major role even though palm jaggery is more nutritious.

4. Diversion of palm sap to alcohol (toddy):

In many regions especially in South India tapping shifted toward kallu (palm wine), reducing the availability of palm sap for jaggery making. This quietly weakened the economic base of palm jaggery during the 1970s–90s. Later, palm wine was banned in Tamil Nadu by 1986 under the Tamil Nadu Prohibition Act, which helped to produce other byproducts of palm.

The sweet comeback of karupatti

Today, the story is changing again. With rising awareness about wholesome foods, diabetes-friendly alternatives, and chemical-free sweeteners, palm jaggery is witnessing renewed demand. Market research by Stellar MR (2024) shows that the global palm sugar market is growing steadily at 3% annually, while India’s jaggery market overall is expanding at over 12% CAGR (IMARC, 2024). As compared to the sugar industry it is still small — but there is growth compared to the previous year of palm production.

In Tamil Nadu, small manufacturers, FPOs, and cottage industries report increased urban demand, especially through online organic stores. This revival is not only cultural but economic — offering new livelihood opportunities for rural communities and palmyrah climbers.

Could palm jaggery trigger an industrial shift?

If palm jaggery production receives:
  • improved safety gear for climbers,
  • modern processing units,
  • government incentives, and
  • branding support,

then Tamil Nadu could build a parallel “traditional sweetener industry” beside the existing sugar economy. Experts say this won’t replace the sugar industry, but it can diversify the state’s sweetener market, stabilise rural incomes, and revive an identity that was once central to Tamil heritage.
