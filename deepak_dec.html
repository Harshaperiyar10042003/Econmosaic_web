Privacy in danger

Interesting fact: Right to information is called a sunshine law because it brings transparency just like sunlight removes darkness

From the very beginning to be precise before eight year itself, the government of India has made it very clear that privacy is a fundamental right. But there has been a long struggle from the government side to create laws to protect people's data. For protecting people's data the government had created digital data personal protection act.(2023), to protect citizens’ personal data. However, this Act did not fully solve the real problems. Many important protections were either removed or weakened, which raised serious concerns among citizens and civil society.Whether this act has solved the existing problem is a big question mark. As this act did not sought to solve the real problem as important protection was removed or weakened. Such weak protection of data undermines trust in digital systems, which is essential for economic stability and growth in a data-driven economy. To find a permanent solution to it the 2023 act was later amended in November 2025 thinking it would bring respite to our existing problem as these changes were presented as improvements, but they largely shifted the focus away from transparency and citizens’ rights. Instead of strengthening the law, these amendments mostly benefited the government and large technology companies, while ordinary people were left with fewer safeguards. sadly the answer was no as the new amended totally back fired on the people who wanted more transparency and privacy. The major problem with these changes is that the rules makes it very easy for the government to access citizens’ personal data, but at the same time, it has become very difficult for citizens to access information from the government. This imbalance has seriously affected transparency. Economically, such an imbalance weakens public trust in institutions, discourages participation in the digital economy, and creates uncertainty for businesses that rely on fair data governance. The DPDP has brought many Changes such as;

The DPDP Act allows the government to:

  1. Exempt its own agencies from data protection obligations
  2. Collect and process personal data for vague reasons such as “public order” or “state interest”. At the same time, citizens are given limited remedies if their data is misused by the State. This creates a situation where power flows only one way—from citizens to the State. This concentration of power negatively impacts economic development by discouraging innovation, startups, and foreign investment, as investors prefer countries with strong and balanced data protection frameworks.

The DPDP has also played vital roles too such as;

  1. It functions under the Ministry of Electronics and Information Technology
  2. Its members are appointed by the government. It lacks clear independence and autonomy. This raises serious concerns about whether the Board can act independently, especially in cases involving government departments or large technology companies.

Instead of being a strong watchdog, the Board risks becoming a rubber-stamp authority, reducing public trust. Weak regulatory institutions increase compliance risks, raise business costs, and reduce confidence in India’s digital regulatory environment. The result of this action is it has totally weakened the right to information (rti) which is one of India's strongest tools for accountability. For example, earlier a citizen could file an RTI asking for the educational qualifications or assets of a public official. This helped people judge whether leaders were qualified and honest.

The public information officers are being authorized to decline any personal information which the public seek to know. At the very end it had wiped transparency. And now, government officials can deny information simply by calling it “personal data. Today. Similarly, information about beneficiaries of government welfare schemes, which was earlier accessible through RTI to check misuse of public money, can now be denied. This directly affects economic efficiency, as reduced scrutiny increases corruption, leakage of public funds, and inefficient allocation of scarce resources. This makes it harder for citizens to expose corruption or wrongdoing. The ultimate outcome of this process is reduced transparency and weaker public accountability.

Adding fire to the fuel, the data protection board (dpb) which handles all the complaints related to data misuse will function under the ministry of electronics and IT. the Board functions under the Ministry of Electronics and Information Technology and its members are appointed by the government.Instead, they give the State and big tech companies more control over citizens’ personal information, while citizens find it increasingly difficult to obtain information from the government.Insecure and unsafe data environments can also create serious economic turmoil. Data breaches, identity theft, financial fraud, and cybercrime increase costs for businesses, reduce consumer confidence, and slow down digital adoption, which is critical for modern economic growth. We are currently at a junction where the new changes in the rule will not be any beneficial to the ordinary citizen. When transparency is reduced, accountability also weakens, and this harms democracy. A system where the government knows more about the people, but people know less about the government, creates an imbalance of power. Such imbalance also distorts economic decision-making, as citizens, markets, and investors operate with incomplete or unreliable information. Ultimately the victim is the ordinary citizen of India as this process gives the state and big companies more control over citizens personal data and the people find it very difficult to get government data putting transparency at a risk. Without trust, transparency, and secure data systems, sustainable economic development becomes difficult, as growth today depends heavily on a trusted digital ecosystem.To correct this situation, clear changes are needed. Personal data must be clearly defined, public interest information should remain accessible through RTI, and the Data Protection Board should be made truly independent. Privacy protection and transparency must work together, not against each other. In conclusion, while protecting personal data is important in the digital age, it should not come at the cost of openness and accountability. A healthy democracy depends on informed citizens, and without transparency, public trust in governance will continue to decline. Without transparency and data security, public trust in governance and markets will continue to decline, ultimately harming India’s long-term economic growth.
