Is India following "development first, ecology later"?
The debate around the great Nicobar project

The Government of India's on 2021 initiated Great Nicobar Island Development Project which triggered intense debate among policymakers, environmentalists, and strategic experts. The project aims to turn Great Nicobar into a major maritime and economic hub and strengthening India's position in Indo pacific region.

Overview of this project:

The Great Nicobar island development project is the mega infrastructure expansion planned in the southern tip of Andaman and Nicobar Islands. This includes 4 key components such as an international container transshipment terminal at Galathea Bay, Greenfield International Airport, greenfield Township Development and 450 MVA gas and solar-based power plant. The initial budget estimated for this project is said to be ₹92,000 crore and the National green tribunal (NGT) approves it. This project is planned to be completed in three phases in over 30 years' time period. The full-fledged infrastructure is to be completed by 2058.

International Container Transshipment Terminal:

At the heart of the plan is an International Container Transshipment Terminal at Galathea Bay. It is situated just 20 Nautical miles (37 km) away from the Malacca Strait, one of the busiest shipping routes in the world. The port is expected to boost India's strategic influence and reduce its dependence on foreign transshipment hubs like Colombo and Singapore. Officials argue that the project will save India millions of dollars (nearly 200-220 million dollars) annually in transshipment costs while strengthening naval logistics and maritime surveillance, as it acts as an operational base for Indian navy and air force. Supporters describe it as a bold step toward making India a stronger player in global supply chains.

Source: Hindustan times

Despite the strategic and economic importance, the project's environmental impacts have raised serious concerns. Great Nicobar is home to fragile coral reef ecosystems, mangroves, and rich biodiversity. It is said that this island was previously listed in Costal regulation zone IA where the ports and other development projects are not allowed due to its most sensitive biodiversity. But in the report released by Ministry of environment, forest and climate change in 2021 this island was listed under CRZ IB which states it is not that sensitive and the ports and other development activities are allowed. Reports indicate that 16,150 out of 20,668 coral colonies (roughly 78%) in the project's impact zone are planned for relocation. And the translocation distance is about 500-550 km which is significantly larger than any other translocations. Coral translocation over long distances carries survival risks, especially in areas already vulnerable to climate change, sea-level rise, and cyclones.

"Environmental advocates argue that short-term economic gains may come at the cost of long-term ecological disaster."

Coral reefs serve as natural coastal barriers, reducing wave energy and protecting shorelines from erosion. If these ecosystems weaken, the island could become more exposed to cyclones and other extreme weather events. As a result, it creates a chain effect where the reef degradation declines the coastal protection, which automatically increases the infrastructure vulnerability and finally it can lead to weaking of strategic sustainability.

Apart from coral degradation, the island was also inhabited by indigenous tribal communities and leatherback turtles which is also has great impact due to this project. Also, millions of trees are to be destroyed.

Looking this project in all these aspects the only question that arise in everyone's mind is that, "can strategic development and ecological sustainability go hand in hand?" As India seeks to expand its maritime influence in the Indo-Pacific, the long-term success of the Great Nicobar project may depend not only on economic returns and strategic gains, but also on how carefully environmental risks are managed.

The future of Great Nicobar now stands at the intersection of strategy and sustainability.

Additional information:
  • The National Green Tribunal (NGT) is a specialized judicial body in India established in 2010 under the National Green Tribunal Act to handle cases related to environmental protection and conservation of natural resources.
  • The Coastal Regulation Zone (CRZ) is a legal framework in India that regulates human and industrial activities along the country's coastline to protect coastal ecosystems such as mangroves, coral reefs, and wetlands. It was first notified in 1991 under the Environment Protection Act, 1986.
