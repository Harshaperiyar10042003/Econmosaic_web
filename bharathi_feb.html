DID THE 18% TARIFF DEAL HURT INDIA? A SYNTHETIC CONTROL ANALYSIS OF THE SHORT-RUN TRADE IMPACT

On February 6, 2026, U.S. and India announced a framework for the most awaited 'Interim Trade Deal' to develop the bilateral trade between these nations. This deal was linked to the US President's executive order removing extra duties on Indian goods. Where the tariff history is as follows,

  • On Aug 2025, Indian exports faced a 50% cumulative tariff burden in the US to punish India for continuing to buy discounted Russian crude oil during the Ukraine war.
  • In early Feb 2025, an executive order was signed by U.S. President Donald J. Trump to remove the additional 25% tariff as a way to improve bilateral trade.
  • During the negotiations on Feb,6, and interim trade talks led to easing of tariffs from 25% to 18%, with the US agreeing to cut duties if India phased down Russian oil purchases.

India's Commerce Minister Piyush Goyal stated that the formal signing of the deal would take place in March, after the text(agreement) was finalized.

What are the Commitments Both the Nations Have Put on the Table for this Deal?

Simply it can be put as, India and the U.S. agreed to cut taxes (tariffs) on each other's goods. India will buy more energy from the U.S. and reduce Russian oil imports. The U.S. removed extra duties on Indian goods and promised fairer trade. Both countries will sign the interim deal in March 2026, and this will be a step toward a improved Bilateral Trade Agreement(BTA).

How the 18% Tariffs Turn to 15%?

On February,20, 2026, The U.S. Supreme Court ruled (6–3) that President Trump did not have authority under International Emergency Economic Powers Act (IEEPA, 1977) to impose enormous global tariffs. The Court said emergency powers cannot be used to levy tariffs in this way. This invalidated the earlier duties (the reduced reciprocal tariff of 18%) and forced the administration to roll them back.

The very next day, President Trump quickly imposed a new 10% global tariff effective from 24 February 2026 on all trading partners, justified as necessary to address U.S. "balance-of-payments deficits". The Section 122 of the Trade Act of 1974 allows the President to impose tariffs up to 15% for 150 days without Congress. Later President Trump raised this to 15%, making it the new baseline duty on imports, including Indian goods.

How does this Lead to Critics on India's Standing on this Deal?

India had already agreed to reduce tariffs on U.S. goods (some to 0%) under the interim deal. After the Supreme Court ruling, critics say India should have renegotiated harder, since the legal basis for high U.S. tariffs was brought down. Instead, Indian exporters still face 15% tariffs, while U.S. exporters enjoy duty-free or reduced access in India. This imbalance is why analysts argue India accepted a weaker position in the deal.

What's India's Reply for the Critics?

India replied that they are not rushing into the final deal. Instead, it paused talks, studying the new legal situation in U.S., and promised to protect its exporters. Officials said India would keep negotiating but will to make sure the deal stayed fair and balanced. The Global Trade Research Institute (GTRI), a Think-Tank, urged India to reassess the deal after the U.S. Court ruling, arguing that the U.S. had lost its legal leverage. India acknowledged this advice and chose to continue negotiations carefully.

What We are Going to Analyze?

When India offered to lower tariffs to 18% in exchange for giving U.S. goods duty-free access, many celebrated it as progress as the Tariff would climb down from 25% to 18%. But was it really a gain for India's economy, or did it weaken our protection against imports?

To answer this, we used the Synthetic Control Method (SCM) - a tool that builds a 'Synthetic India' by combining trade patterns of similar countries like Vietnam, Thailand, Indonesia, and China. This allowed us to create a counterfactual scenario: what if India had kept its 25% tariffs instead of signing the deal?

Our aim was not to prove an absolute truth, but to provide an estimated picture of how India's trade balance might have look under different choices. By comparing "Real India" with "Synthetic India," we can estimate whether the deal protected our exporters or exposed them to new risks.

How We Built Synthetic India (Pre-Supreme Court Scenario)

This analysis was carried out before the U.S. Supreme Court ruling in February 2026. At that time, India had agreed to lower tariffs to 18%, while U.S. goods were set to enter with 0% or reduced tariffs. Our model is built on this pre-ruling scenario, so it does not reflect the later change when President Trump reset tariffs to 15% under a different law.

We built "Synthetic India" by combining trade data from Vietnam, Thailand, Indonesia, and China. These countries were chosen because they are India's direct competitors in manufacturing and had not signed a similar deal. We used trade balance patterns (exports minus imports) as the main measure based on which this model was built.

Based on the model result, India's "digital twin" can be described as it is built with 59.2% of Vietnam, 19.4% of Thailand, 17% of Indonesia, and 4% China. The Predictor Balance values, indicates that before the deal, India's actual trade balance and Synthetic India's trade balance were almost identical (-3855 vs -3844). This gave us confidence that the model was a good approximation. The RMSPE (error budget) was 1764, which is low enough to suggest the model is reliable, but not perfect.

What does the Graph Shows?

In the graph, the blue line (Actual India with the 18% deal) and the red dashed line (Synthetic India with 25% tariffs) move almost together for years. This tells us that before the deal, India's trade balance was very similar to what our model predicted.

But, towards the end of 2025, the two lines in the graph, begin to move away. The synthetic path stays relatively stable. In contrast, the real India path drops sharply into a deeper trade deficit. This means that after the tariff joint statement release or the expectation on the trade deal, leads India's imports to increase much faster than its exports, creating a larger gap in the trade balance.

Official trade balance data was available only up to December 2025, while the tariff deal was signed in February 2026. To cover the gap, our model made short-run projections for January to March 2026 using past trade trends. These are precautionary estimates, not exact predictions — they suggest what might happen if the same dynamics continued, but they cannot account for unexpected shocks like oil price changes, political decisions, or sudden demand shifts.

Interpreting the Results:
  • January 2026: The model shows that if India had kept 25% tariffs, the trade deficit would have been about –$8.66 billion. In reality, after the 18% deal, India's deficit jumped to –$34.68 billion. That's a gap of roughly –$26 billion, meaning imports rose much faster than exports could handle.
  • February 2026: The gap remained large, with the real deficit at –$34 billion compared to the synthetic estimate of –$8.95 billion. The difference was still about –$25 billion.
  • March 2026: The real deficit improved slightly to –$31 billion, but the synthetic estimate was –$9.23 billion, leaving a gap of about –$22 billion.

The tariff deal was officially signed in February 2026, but the sharp gap showing up in January 2026 reflects how markets often react in advance. Traders and importers had already anticipated the agreement because negotiations and announcements were underway, so they began adjusting shipments earlier. This expectation led to a surge in imports before the formal start date, while exports did not rise at the same pace. In simple terms, January's numbers capture the anticipation effect of the deal, while February and March show the actual short-term impact after the tariffs were imposed.

Limitations:
  • The model assumes that the comparison countries (Vietnam, Thailand, Indonesia, China) were not affected by India's tariff deal, but in reality, global trade changes can spill over.
  • The study only looks at a short period after the deal (January–March 2026), so the results mainly show short-term reactions and may longer-term effects may differ.
  • Other important factors like exchange rates, oil prices, or global demand shifts were not included, even though they can strongly affect trade balances.
  • The results depend on the countries and variables chosen for the model; using different inputs could give different numbers.

This analysis should be seen as an educational estimate of possible outcomes, not a final or absolute judgment on policy.

A Short-Run Estimate, not a Final Verdict

Our analysis shows that India's trade deficit in January 2026 could have been about $26 billion smaller if the old 25% tariffs had stayed in place. But this is only a short-term estimate, not proof that the policy failed. The method relies on assumptions and looks only at the first few months after the deal, when trade usually reacts sharply before settling. Over time, companies adjust supply chains, renegotiate contracts, and reorganize production, which can change the picture.

So, these results should be seen as an educational short-run exercise, not a final judgment. While the early numbers show a bigger deficit, India's decision may still prove beneficial in the longer term as exporters adapt and new opportunities open up.
