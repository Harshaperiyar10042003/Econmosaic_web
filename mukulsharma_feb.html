Revival of the Global Organisation to address environmental governance

Mukul Sharma

In the research article, we will discuss the need to revive the UN Trusteeship Council. What were the original mandates and new mandates that we have to amend for the purpose of recalibrating with the global commons and the future road ahead? In the 21st Century, the need to rejuvenate global organisations like the United Nations in light of differing political interests and to maintain the security of the global commons. It is crucial to restructure and evolve the United Nations with an understanding that there are places, territories and areas that require careful nurturing for our better future.

In 2021, the United Nations Secretary-General, Antonio Guterres, in his report "Our Common Agenda", suggested that the UNTC should be repurposed as a deliberative forum. Similar suggestions were made in another report, "Our common agenda and the road to 2023," which stated, "the need to improve governance of the global commons." These reports came as a ray of hope, when the world was preparing to celebrate the 50th Anniversary of the 1972 UN Conference on the Human Environment (UNCHE), that is, on 2nd and 3rd of June, 2022. The Stockholm 50+ Conference was organised, which also emphasised the need to protect and, where possible, improve governance of the global commons.

As we know, the Trusteeship Council was established as one of the principal organs of the United Nations under Chapter 13 of the Charter. It was designed to supervise the trust territories under individual agreements. UNTC suspended its operations on the 10th November 1994, following the independence of the last trust territory, Palau. Articles 86 and 87 talk about the membership and administrative functioning of the Trusteeship Council, respectively. Under Article 88, UNTC submits a report to the United Nations on the political, economic, social, and educational advancement of each trust territory.

Thus, as indicated above, the Trusteeship Council can start functioning again by amending its rules and procedures. But here two questions arise: first, how to reorient and repurpose the organisation, and second, what should constitute the new mandate? The UNTC could be entrusted with supervising the fragmented legal regimes governing environmental protection and the global commons. Rather, it should share the tasks of the other two overburdened principal organs, such as the General Assembly and the ECOSOC (Economic and Social Council). In 1998, the UN Secretary General Kofi Annan and the chairperson of the Task Force Klaus Topfer made a relevant reference in their note 'A new concept of Trusteeship'. To support the same Political Scholar, Edith Brown Weiss has articulated the concept of intergenerational equity, which considers members of the present generation to hold the earth 'in trust' for future generations. The UN report adopted in 1995 suggests that the Trusteeship Council should be given a new mandate over the global commons, in light of concerns for the security of the planet.

The 'global Commons' here refers to the resource domains which are beyond the political reach of any state. In Modern International Law, four major areas are covered: the high seas, the atmosphere, Antarctica, and outer space. Hence, the main concern remains environmental protection, as well as the sustainable use and conservation of resources in the global commons.

Here is another question which arises: Can the Trusteeship Council be revived and repurposed with a new mandate? In view of the possibility of the revival of the TC, an amendment of Chapter 13 of the UN Charter is necessary. According to the scholarly research by (Prof.) Bharat H Desai, In terms of composition, the council should be a small body with limited membership; If retained in its present form, UNEP would have to work out a special agreement with ECOSOC. If this happens, UNEP, as a 'specialised agency', would report directly to the Trusteeship Council and through it to the UN General Assembly.

The revived UNTC's global supervision assumes significance for areas that hold special value for the present and future well-being of humankind. The dispute over territorial claims in Antarctica, the claims to atmospheric rights, and the regulation of areas beyond the limits of national jurisdiction hold a ray of hope for a better, more common environmental future on the planet. This could strengthen the UN and underscore the core purpose for which the United Nations was founded in 1945.
