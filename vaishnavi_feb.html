Budgetary Tug-of-War: Defence Imperative vs. Educational Mission

- By Vaishnavi

In the case of a nation like India, which finds itself in a sensitive geopolitical environment while at the same time hosting the youth of the world, the allocation of resources between the defence budget and the education budget is a classic problem of balance. It might appear like a strange allocation, but Defence always gets a much larger share of the pie than Education. However, looking closely at the legal and economic needs that are based on short-term as well as long-term development imperatives reveals a complex rationale.

Fortified Shield: A Review of Defense Spending vis-à-vis Education

A nation-state's primary responsibility is to protect its people and territorial sovereignty. The stability required for businesses, educational institutions, and economic growth is jeopardised in the absence of security guarantees. The 2026 budget continues the dominant role of the defence sector, founded on three fundamental principles:

Geopolitical Necessities: India has very large, highly active, and highly sensitive borders that extend over thousands of kilometres. The imperative to maintain a constant two-front preparedness position requires constant modernisation. While an education policy can be adjusted over a period of a decade, an error in security policy poses an immediate threat to existence.

High Cost of Modernisation: Defence inherently involves high capital costs. The expenditures required to maintain a fleet of domestically constructed aircraft carriers or acquire a single squadron of fifth-generation fighter jets are significantly higher than those required to establish several universities. Additionally, the Aatmanirbhar Bharat scheme put immense focus on indigenous research and development. Even though this strategy reduces future import costs, the massive initiative supporting the Indian Semiconductor Mission shows the high initial investment required for the shift to indigenous production.

The Sunk Cost of Maintenance: A large part of the Defence Budget is comprised of sunk costs, such as pension and salary payments to millions of active and retired personnel. This is a contractual and social commitment that cannot be easily abandoned without significant reform.

The Silent Engine: The Strategic Rise of Education

While defence represents the shield that protects the present, education represents the engine that drives the future. Although the education budget has always comprised a smaller percentage of GDP (around 3-4%) compared to the 6% target outlined in the National Education Policy (NEP) 2020, the 2026 budget represents a paradigm shift rather than a simple scaling-up. The government follows a policy focused on "Human Capital Transformation", aiming for a transition from "rote learning" to a "skill-based economy". In light of the fact that India's formal skill level is presently estimated at a relatively low 4.7%, the budget for 2026 focuses on the following:

Digital Infrastructure: Closing the gap between rural and urban areas by ensuring high-speed internet connectivity in government schools.

Vocational Training: Aligning the curriculum with industry needs to resolve the "unemployability" of graduates.

Research and Innovation: Allocating resources to specialised institutions to ensure that the next "Aatmanirbhar" products are designed by Indian researchers.

Comparative Analysis: Security versus Sustainability

The higher allocation to the defence sector can be explained, to some extent, by the nature of the resources involved. Tanks and missile defence systems have high unit costs and a short shelf life, requiring constant financial outlays. The education sector, on the other hand, requires large investments in infrastructure but is less dependent on capital expenditure in the short term.

Way Forward: Security via Knowledge

For a country to truly be powerful, its defence systems must be accompanied by a scientifically aware and educated population. The Union Budget 2026 shows a governmental effort to target a 4.3% fiscal deficit, thus ensuring disciplined spending.

As the country moves towards 2047 (Viksit Bharat), the gap between the two allocations will gradually close. A secure boundary will be of little use if the domestic population is unable to compete in the globalised digital economy. A country that cannot protect its physical interests will have a hard time supporting people who are highly educated. The people who are highly educated will find it difficult to thrive in this kind of country.

Did you know?

India is one of the five countries in the world when it comes to spending money on the military and also home to one of the higher education systems in the world. The target of NEP 2020 is to spend double the amount of public spending on education and nearly 6% of India's GDP.
