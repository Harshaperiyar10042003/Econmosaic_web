The Social Political Analysis of Water Conflicts

Water is essential for life. But, it causes both cooperation and conflict which further take the shape of social, political and technological debates. The global water issue, rooted largely in human caused factors, disproportionately hits marginalized groups the most. Framed through the idea of water stress, the need is for hydraulic justice - a just water order that prioritizes community wisdom over technocratic approaches.

Case Study - Plachimada, Kerala

Since 1998, Coca-Cola has extracted about 2 million litres per day, using 3.8 litres to make 1 litres of beverage. This resulted in water scarcity, health issues, pollution and dumping of sludge in public areas. Villagers resisted socially and legally; Kerala High court ruled on writ petition filed by panchayat, the groundwater is a public resource and therefore, can not be exploited for private gain. The legal battle continued through appeals and counter-appeals. World Water Conference held near Plachimada at Pudussery in 2004, it stated that water preservation is a fundamental obligation and rejected water commodification, marketisation and corporation.

Sociological Understanding of the Crisis

Historically, societies adapted to water challenges but adaptation is now constrained by water’s finiteness and irreplaceability. Over-reliance on technological solutions can marginalise traditional knowledge and community inputs. Reductionist science and globalised technical “solutions” often distort local realities, reinforcing exploitative economic systems. Effective strategies must be guided by differential epistemology which involves adjusting solutions to special social, cultural and economic situations.

Challenges

As per Dublin Principles (1992), there is a link between water usage efficiency and pricing. But, defining water as an economic good, can disproportionately harm the poor and leads to equal wastefulness. There is a risk that vulnerable communities may be “pricing out”, compromising equal water access. Hydraulic Injustice occurs when marginalised groups bear the brunt of scarcity while governance ignores their needs. Present models privilege technical over the political, ignoring socio-cultural contexts. Climate change and Anthropogenic pressure on resources like urbanisation, industrialization, intensified agriculture and rising sea levels can damage and deplete fresh water.

Measures

Hydraulic Justice which means ethical, participatory water governance ensures equitable access combines natural science with social science insights to integrate ecological, cultural, political and economic perspectives. Avoiding universal technical fixes and tailor policies to each society’s socio-economic and differential epistemology. Recognize water as a fundamental right categorizing groundwater and other sources as public trust resources.

Did you know?

Only 3% of Earth’s water is fresh, and less than 1% of that water is accessible for daily use; the rest is locked in ice form or deep underground.
