Doughnut Economics

We're often told that a healthy economy is one that is always growing. But what if endless growth is pushing our planet beyond its breaking point, while still leaving millions of people in need? What if, instead of chasing a bigger GDP, we aimed for an economy that is both ecologically safe and socially just? This is the transformative goal of Doughnut Economics.

What Is Doughnut Economics?

In simple terms, Doughnut Economics is a visual framework and a new mindset for guiding our economies. Created by economist Kate Raworth, it pictures a sustainable economy as a "doughnut" the sweet spot between two rings.

  • The inner ring represents the social foundation the minimum standards of life every person deserves, like food, water, healthcare, education, housing, and political voice. Falling short of this ring means people face deprivation.
  • The outer ring is the ecological ceiling the planetary boundaries we must not overshoot, like climate change, biodiversity loss, and pollution. Breaking through this ceiling causes environmental collapse.
  • The doughnut itself—the safe and just space between these rings is where humanity can thrive without destabilizing the planet.

The bottom line is to make the economy regenerative and distributive by design, ensuring no one is left in the hole in the middle and no one pushes beyond the outer limits.

The Economics Behind It

Traditional economics, focused on perpetual GDP growth, often treats social inequality and environmental damage as afterthoughts or "externalities." The Doughnut model flips this. It argues that the primary goal of 21st-century economics should be to bring all of humanity into that safe and just space.

Think of it as a compass for progress. Instead of asking, "How can we grow more?" it asks, "How can we ensure everyone's needs are met without overshooting Earth's limits?" This shifts policy focus from mere production to regeneration (like circular economies that reuse materials) and distribution (ensuring wealth, knowledge, and power are shared more fairly).

The model has moved from theory to practice. Amsterdam became the first city to officially adopt the Doughnut as a tool for city planning post COVID, using it to guide decisions on circular construction, energy, and fair wages. Brussels and other cities worldwide are now following suit.

Challenges and Opportunities

The biggest challenge is measurement and systemic change. Our entire global system from corporate governance to international trade is wired for growth, not for balance. Moving to a Doughnut model requires rethinking laws, business models, and how we define success.

However, its power lies in offering a positive, holistic vision. For a country like India, the Doughnut provides a crucial lens. It frames the development challenge not just as lifting people out of poverty (raising the social foundation) but doing so through clean energy, sustainable agriculture, and resilient cities (respecting the ecological ceiling). This avoids the outdated "grow first, clean up later" path.

It's interesting to mention that Kate Raworth first published the idea as a discussion paper for Oxfam in 2012. This humble, visually intuitive beginning has sparked a global movement among city planners, activists, and forward-thinking businesses, redefining what a successful economy looks like.
