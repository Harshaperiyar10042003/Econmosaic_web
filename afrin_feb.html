FISCAL PRUDENCE AND SUSTAINABLE GROWTH: BALANCING DOMESTIC PRIORITIES WITH INTERNATIONAL STANDARDS IN BUDGET 2025–26

The Union Budget 2025-26 of India, along with the Economic Survey project a wholesome economic framework that aims at balancing the local developmental requirements with the internationally recognized ideals of financial accountability and macroeconomic stability. When the global economy is facing greater levels of uncertainty regarding geopolitical confrontations, trade protectionism, and a volatile capital market, India has taken a policy route that promotes growth-driven state investment and demonstrates restraint in fiscal policy. The latter two-pronged approach demonstrates the governmental adherence to sustainable long-term development in accordance with the international best practices.


One of the key aspects that can be drawn out of the Economic Survey is that the growth potential of India has been increasing steadily. The Survey upgrades India's medium-term potential growth rate to 7 per cent with reference to continuous infrastructure development, enhanced logistics, and a steady public investment rate. This is notable since most of the developed and developing economies are experiencing a slowdown in their economies. It is in this international context that policy orientation, as depicted by India's policy of ensuring macroeconomic stability as it pursues rapid expansion of productive investment, is a balanced one. The economic strategy of the Budget is based on fiscal consolidation. The government has once again restated that it will bring down the fiscal deficits gradually and at the same time not cut the spending that is needed to boost growth. The practice is reflective of international best practices in which careful management of government funds is deemed critical towards preserving investor trust and economic probity. The Economic Survey highlights that India has gained a stable platform to grow, in spite of the world turmoil, due to strong macroeconomic fundamentals, such as contained inflation, healthy banking systems, and robust corporate balance sheets. Such stability is crucial in attracting long-term investment and protecting the economy against foreign shocks.


Infrastructure expenditure has been placed on the centre stage by the growth strategy. The huge investments in transport systems, energy, and digital networks aim to boost productivity and ease structural bottlenecks. The Survey notes that the increased airport, inland waterways, and logistics growth in the past decade have contributed immensely to economic efficiency. These programs are not contrary to the international practice where infrastructure-related development has turned out to be an economic development driver. The budget is formulated using international standards that promote expenditure in long-term productive things by laying emphasis on capital expense as opposed to revenue expenditure. Competitiveness in the manufacturing and export sectors is another crucial element of the fiscal policy.


The Budget proclaims the National Mission on Manufacturing, which aims to increase the contribution of the manufacturing sector to GDP to 25 per cent by 2035, and increase the number of jobs and merchandise exports to USD 1.2 trillion. This is a structural change towards the development of domestic capabilities and making India more integrated into the global value chains. This is at a time when the world is getting fragmented in trade and protectionist inclinations are on the rise. The Indian approach aims at increasing resiliency but is outward-looking.


The fiscal policy external sector dimension is also taken care of. The outflows of global foreign direct investment slowed by 11 per cent in 2024, but India is still receiving relatively stable outflows because of the consistency of the policy and market potential. The agreement between India and EFTA in the recent past, which is expected to bring in USD 100 billion investment, serves another advantage to India in terms of its external economic position. The emphasis of the Budget on the ease of conducting business and simplification of regulations is thus geared towards maintaining investor confidence in the competitive global environment.


Sustainable development does not concern the industry and trade only. The agriculture industry and the related industries have been performing steadily with an average growth rate of 4.4 per cent in the past five years. This type of sector-wide development will mean that fiscal policy promotes an inclusive development, as opposed to urban or industrial specific growth. The government sustains the social stability, which is an essential element of sustainable macroeconomic management, by keeping rural incomes constant and enhancing food security. The other pillar of the government strategy is financial sector reforms. The emergence of the International Financial Services Centre in GIFT City indicates India's desire to match the global standard of a financial ecosystem. By the end of 2025, GIFT City will have over 1,034 domestic and foreign organisations in its location, controlling the banking assets worth USD 100 billion and gaining momentum in its global financial rating. The creation of such a globalized financial infrastructure to encourage effective mobilisation of capital and the observation of international regulatory and governance standards.


Innovation and human capital are also noted as key elements to long-term sustainability in the Budget. 2019 is the 66th, and 2025 is the 38th by position, with a notable increase in the Global Innovation Index, which signifies the impact of more competition in research and technology.


More investments in education, skilling, and digital transformation will assist in producing a labor force that can support the global industry standards. Such measures are measures that internalize so that fiscal consolidation is not at the cost of future-oriented investments.


More importantly, the government has not been involved in short term populism activities that can undermine fiscal stability. It has rather pursued structural adjustments such as rationalisation of taxes, liberalisation of key sectors, as well as streamlining of procedures through regulation. According to the Economic Survey, the reforms being made include the GST reforms, labour reforms, and foreign investment policy, among others, as an indicator of the additional commitment to improving the efficiency of the economy. This reform is vital in ensuring that the public funds can be sustained in the long run.


In conclusion, the Union Budget 2025-26 has been the representation of a responsible economic policy that does not attempt to achieve excessive growth, yet it is not economically irresponsible. India is up to the international standard of good economic governance through the emphasis on infrastructure projects, encouragement of manufacturing expansion, strengthening the financial sector, and macroeconomic discipline. In a world where doubt is on the rise, the Indian approach shows that it is possible to achieve sustainable development through an element of national reform and good interaction with the external world. The Budget, thus, puts India not just as a stable economy but also as an emerging powerhouse that is determined to grow responsibly and inclusively in the global economy.

Did you know?

GIFT City stands for Gujarat International Finance Tec-City. It is India's first and only International Financial Services Centre (IFSC), located in Gandhinagar, Gujarat. The project was developed with the vision of creating a globally competitive financial and technology hub within India, comparable to international financial centres like Singapore, Dubai, London, and Hong Kong.


Before GIFT City, many Indian companies conducted global financial transactions from abroad. GIFT City was designed to reverse this outflow and bring those activities back to India.
