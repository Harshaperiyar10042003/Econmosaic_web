Is New Excise Duty on Cigarettes is concerning about human's health:

From February 2026, cigarettes and tobacco products will attract an additional excise duty and cess and above the highest 40% in GST slab, it replaces the earlier structure of 28% GST plus compensation cess. The suggestion of WHO (World Health Organization) is that the current tax burden is not sufficient on the tobacco industry because they recommend increasing the tax on tobacco products to a minimum of 75% as concerning of Humans Health. As a result of this new tax regime, it is expected that cigarette prices will increase up to Rs. 55 per pack. The pack of 10 sticks is now costing at least 22 – 25, more than before. The increase in cigarette prices will vary depending on the length and type of cigarette. On the point of view of distributors, "Premium cigarettes of 76mm length will now cost Rs. 50 – 55 per pack more than before, which depends upon the brand, and a packet of "Wills navy cut 76mm" was earlier priced at Rs. 95 per pack of 10 sticks, but now it is expected to cost of Rs. 120 per pack. The length of a 84mm cigarette (Gold flake, Kings, Lights, Wills Classic and Wills Classic mild's) is priced at Rs. 170 per pack of 10 sticks. Now it will cost Rs. 220 – 225 per pack.

So now we can see the benefits of the new excise duty on cigarettes. In public finance it is called the tobacco tax, which is a Sin tax. Because the increasing taxes can discourage consumption and internalize negative externalities, the private cost of smoking is decreasing than the social cost. It leads to overconsumption of cigarettes, but the increase in excise duty helps to correct the market failure, and generate government revenue. Tobacco Products are a large contributor to the Government's Tax Revenue. According to The Tobacco Institute of India, tax revenue from tobacco products is more than Rs.76,000 crores annually, among which, legal cigarettes contribute a very high proportion of the tobacco tax revenue as cigarettes bear the brunt of taxation in India. If you see it from a public health economics perspective, the new excise duty can reduce health care expenses indirectly, increase life expectancy, and boost workforce productivity. But the problem is, cigarettes and tobacco items have a relatively inelastic demand, meaning consumers will continue to buy the products at any level of cost. It won't reduce consumption in the short run because of addiction, and it will become a habit also.

India has a unique pattern of tobacco consumption, the Legal Cigarettes account in India for just 10% of overall tobacco consumed. The rest of the other 90% consumption is in the form of 29 tax - inefficient tobacco products such as bidis, chewing tobacco, khaini etc. Although most tobacco products fall under the 28% GST slab, the effective tax burden varies significantly across products. Manufactured cigarettes attract substantially higher total taxation due to additional excise duties and cess, whereas bidis and certain smokeless tobacco products face relatively lower effective tax incidence and compliance levels, and low revenue relative to their consumption levels.

In this table we can see the condition of India's overall tobacco consumption, which includes legal cigarettes and other tobacco, including illegal cigarettes. Over the last three decades, the share of legal cigarette consumption in India has declined from 21% in 1981–82 to 10% in 2023 – 2024. Parallelly, other tobacco consumption in India has increased 79% in 1981 – 82 to 90% in 2023 – 24. The overall tobacco consumption in India has increased by 49%, it means the consumers are shifted to the illegal cigarettes and other tax inefficient tobacco products in India. According to the WHO estimates 2010–2015, there are 350 million smokeless tobacco users in the world, a substantial 70% of them are in India. While the Legal Cigarette industry in India is the organized sector with strict regulations, the bulk of other tobacco consumed is largely produced in the unorganized sector, which does not have any regulations. This large unorganized sector, estimated at 68% of overall tobacco consumption, pays little tax due to tax exemptions or evasion.

Suggestions:

If you see in the aspect of the Indian government, they will say this new excise duty can help them in many ways, like reduce the consumption and improved the health and life expectancy etc. While the government justifies higher excise duties on cigarettes as a public health intervention, some observers contend that the policy may also influence by revenue consideration, especially since legal cigarettes contribute a substantial share of tobacco tax revenue, if they really want to protect the health of Indian people, they can increase the taxes on other or tax inefficient tobaccos, or they can try to ban the items like pan masala, Guttkah, in India some states even banned these items. So, they can strictly put restrictions on these harmful things, but it may not be an easy thing, because the major tobacco growing states are Gujarat, Andhra, Karnataka, Uttar Pradesh and Bihar. According to recent FAO estimates for 2022, India ranks as the second-largest producer of tobacco after China (FCV used for cigarettes, bidi, hookah, Chewing tobacco). Then taxation alone may not be sufficient to reduce tobacco consumption, particularly given the addictive nature of these products. Therefore, a multidimensional policy approach, including behavioural intervention like creating campaigns and providing more awareness in their schooling period, and restrictions for people below 18 years old, may be more effective in achieving long-term reductions in tobacco use.
