Union Budget 2026: Key Concerns and Criticisms

India has finalized its Annual Financial Statement for the financial year 2026–27, presenting it as a youth-driven budget. The budget is predominantly oriented toward long-term development rather than short-term relief measures. While it promises several benefits for the public, it also reveals certain major and minor shortcomings in areas such as allocation, taxation, and programme implementation. Despite being formulated by the government in consultation with leading economists, some of these gaps appear to have been either intentionally or unintentionally overlooked.

1. Burden for Middle Class

The revised Income Tax slabs announced in 2025 came into effect from April 2026 for the financial year 2026–27. Under the new tax regime, individuals are eligible for an effective tax exemption up to ₹12 lakh, along with a standard deduction of ₹75,000 for salaried taxpayers. This provides significant relief to middle- and upper-middle-income earners by increasing their disposable income. However, while lower-income individuals may be exempt from direct income tax, they still pay indirect taxes such as GST on goods and services at rates similar to higher-income groups. Since indirect taxes are largely uniform regardless of income level, this creates a proportional burden on lower earners. This structural feature of the tax system is often viewed as a limitation in ensuring complete tax equity. The government expects that higher disposable income will increase savings and investment. However, people may instead choose to increase their consumption, especially if they prefer present spending over future saving.

2. GST

Under the GST framework, unbranded and unpackaged rice remains exempt, while pre-packaged and labelled rice attracts 5% GST. However, ready-to-use idli and dosa batter, when classified under HSN 2106 as a prepared food product, attracts 18% GST. This distinction between basic food grains and value-added preparations has generated debate, particularly in South India, where such items are daily staples. The core issue lies in classification. Minor differences in processing, packaging, or branding can shift an item from a lower tax slab to a significantly higher one. Such inconsistencies create ambiguity and raise concerns about fairness and regional neutrality in indirect taxation. Greater clarity and uniformity in GST classification of essential food items would help reduce confusion and strengthen public confidence in the tax system.

3. Rare Earth Programme

The government allocated ₹7,280 crore in 2025 for the Rare Earth Programme to boost exploration, extraction, and processing of strategic minerals. Rare earth corridors are planned in Andhra Pradesh, Telangana, Kerala, and Odisha. The programme targets critical materials such as neodymium, tantalum, cobalt, lanthanum, scandium, and tellurium for their use in technology, defence, and clean energy sectors. In Odisha, particularly in coastal and tribal regions such as Ganjam, mining activities may lead to land conflicts, forest degradation, and displacement of tribal communities, along with pollution from mineral processing plants. In Kerala, especially in districts like Kollam and Alappuzha, the impact could be more severe due to the already fragile coastline, where mining may accelerate coastal erosion, disrupt fishing livelihoods, affect tourism, and create waste management challenges. In Tamil Nadu, areas such as Thoothukudi and Kanyakumari may face ecological disturbances from beach sand mining, leading to protests from fishing communities due to income loss, along with potential health risks associated with radioactive elements present in monazite. Similarly, in Andhra Pradesh, coastal districts like Srikakulam and Nellore may experience coastal erosion from beach sand mining, loss of livelihood for fishing communities, groundwater contamination, and possible radiation risks linked to mineral processing activities.

4. Railway Routes

In the Union Budget 2026–27, the Indian government announced plans to develop seven new high-speed rail (bullet train) corridors to modernize long-distance travel, calling them "growth connectors" for major economic regions. These proposed corridors are: Mumbai–Pune, Pune–Hyderabad, Hyderabad–Bengaluru, Hyderabad–Chennai, Chennai–Bengaluru, Delhi–Varanasi, and Varanasi–Siliguri. However, there are concerns about who will primarily benefit from these projects. Bullet trains typically have higher ticket prices, which may make them less accessible to low-income passengers, while many ordinary travellers continue to rely on crowded unreserved coaches in conventional trains. In some cases, prioritization of high-speed services could also affect the scheduling and efficiency of regular passenger trains, raising questions about whether such investments align with the objectives of a welfare-oriented public transport system.

5. AIIMS

The government allocated significant funds under the Pradhan Mantri Swasthya Suraksha Yojana (PMSSY) in the 2025–26 budget to strengthen tertiary healthcare infrastructure, including the establishment of new AIIMS institutions. However, building new facilities should not be the only goal; monitoring progress and ensuring timely completion are essential government responsibilities. For example, the proposed AIIMS in Darbhanga (Bihar) and AIIMS Madurai (Tamil Nadu) remain under construction with work progressing slowly, and AIIMS in Majra (Haryana) is also yet to be fully completed despite earlier announcements. While infrastructure expansion can improve healthcare access, efficient execution, appropriate location planning, and faster implementation are necessary to translate budgetary allocations into real benefits for the public.

6. Securities Transaction Tax

To curb excessive speculation in the derivatives market, the government increased the Securities Transaction Tax (STT) on futures to 0.05% and on options to 0.15%. This revision primarily affects short-term domestic traders and frequent market participants who rely heavily on derivative instruments. While the move is intended to discourage speculative volatility and promote market stability, it raises concerns among active traders regarding increased transaction costs.

7. Sovereign Gold Bonds

A major issue in the Budget 2026 update on Sovereign Gold Bonds (SGBs) is that it reduces their attractiveness in the secondary market. Earlier, investors benefited from tax exemption if they held the bond until maturity. Now, only the original buyers who hold the bond for the full 8 years receive the tax benefit. Anyone who buys the bond later from the stock exchange must pay 12.5% long-term capital gains tax. Because of this, new buyers may demand a lower price to compensate for the future tax they must pay. This reduces the resale value of the bonds and makes it harder for original investors to sell them before maturity. In simple terms, the same bond now has different value depending on who buys it and how it was purchased, which weakens liquidity in the market.

8. Tax Holidays for Foreign Technology Companies

Tax holidays and large incentives offered to foreign companies in advanced sectors such as AI and cloud computing may create important structural concerns for India. While these measures aim to attract investment and promote technological growth, they also reduce government revenue at a time when fiscal discipline under the FRBM framework requires deficit control, potentially limiting spending on health, education, and infrastructure. Large foreign technology firms may gain significant market power due to their financial strength and data control, making competition difficult for domestic startups and MSMEs. Moreover, AI and cloud investments are largely capital-intensive and automation-driven, generating relatively limited direct employment compared to the scale of incentives provided. Concerns also arise regarding profit repatriation and data control, which may affect India's digital sovereignty if core digital infrastructure remains under foreign ownership. Overdependence on foreign technology providers could slow domestic innovation and create long-term technological dependence, making short-term investment gains potentially costly for sustainable economic development.

View of Major Economists

Knowledgeable experts have sharply criticised the Budget speech. Dr Surjit Bhalla ridiculed the self-congratulation. About the fourth largest economy, Dr C Rangarajan called out the slow pace of fiscal consolidation. Dr Ashok Gulati deplored the neglect of large parts of the farm sector. Professor Rohit Lamba (Cornell University) mocked the Budget as fit for an economy in search of a plan. The Finance Minister threw schemes, programmes, missions, institutes, initiatives, Funds, Committees, etc at her listeners. I counted at least 24. They will soon find out that no money was allocated for many of these announcements! The Budget was an exercise in intellectual laziness.

Conclusion

In my view, the Union Budget 2026 is a well-intentioned and growth-oriented budget with a strong focus on long-term development, particularly in infrastructure and modernization. However, its long-term orientation may not fully address the immediate needs of lower-income groups, who form a significant part of India's workforce and economic foundation. As India continues to grow, development should remain inclusive and balanced across all sections of society. While infrastructure expansion enhances economic capacity and national image, equal importance should be given to social welfare, environmental sustainability, and improvements in everyday public services. Although programmes such as Swachh Bharat have contributed to cleanliness efforts, continued emphasis on sanitation, waste management, environmental protection, and civic awareness remains essential. Sustainable progress requires not only visible infrastructural development but also improvements in living standards, environmental quality, and public responsibility.
